Trust & Liability Guardrails

A plain-language reference for the founder, the Arizona side, and anyone writing words a client will read: what LLV does, what it doesn't, and why.

## 1. The One Idea

LLV is a record-keeper, not a promise-maker. We keep careful, trustworthy records of our clients' belongings, and we move and care for those belongings. We do not make promises about whether an item is genuine or what it is worth — because a promise you can be wrong about is exactly what gets a small company sued. Everything below comes from that single idea.

## 2. Two Simple Pictures

**The coat-check, not the appraiser.** A coat-check says: "You handed me this coat. Here's your ticket. I'll return it exactly as you gave it to me, and I wrote down when it came in and when it went out." It never says "this is a genuine designer coat." An appraiser does say that — and is on the hook if it's wrong. LLV is the coat-check with an excellent logbook.

**The notebook, not the jeweler.** Writing down what you paid for your jewelry in a notebook is harmless. Hiring a jeweler to officially declare what it is worth is a professional, regulated job with real responsibility attached. LLV holds the notebook. LLV never becomes the jeweler.

## 3. The Chanel Story — Why This Matters

In 2018, the fashion house Chanel sued the resale company The RealReal. The heart of the complaint was simple: The RealReal told shoppers that items were "authentic," and Chanel argued that some were not genuine and that The RealReal was not qualified to make that call. That lawsuit is still going in 2026 — roughly eight years of legal cost for a large, well-funded public company.

The lesson for LLV: the moment you claim to verify that luxury goods are genuine, two doors open at once. A customer can come after you if you are wrong, and the brand itself can come after you simply for making the claim. LLV avoids both by never making the claim.

## 4. What LLV Will NOT Do — and Why

For each of these, the reason is the same: it would turn LLV into a promise-maker and create a risk a small pilot cannot survive.

| What LLV will NOT do | The main downside if it did | What LLV does instead |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Claim an item is genuine ("authenticate" it). | If we are wrong, the loss is partly our fault because the client trusted our word — and luxury brands sue companies that claim to authenticate their products (the Chanel case). | Keep and show the authenticity papers the owner already has. Make no claim of our own. |
| Put our own dollar value on an item ("appraise" it). | Clients make insurance, sale, and estate decisions on that number. If it is wrong, we can be blamed — and officially valuing things is a licensed, regulated job. | Record what the owner says it is worth, clearly labeled "owner-provided." Leave official numbers to licensed appraisers or insurers. |
| Treat a client's belongings as an "investment portfolio" we grow. | That language makes LLV sound like a financial advisor managing investments — one of the most heavily regulated activities there is. | Talk about a wardrobe and its records: condition, history, and where things are. |
| Sell clients' items or set resale prices (in the pilot). | Selling on someone's behalf and setting prices is where resale companies end up in disputes, plus money-handling risk the pilot is not built for. | Leave resale to a later phase — owner-controlled, and ideally through a partner who carries that risk. |

## 5. What LLV WILL Confidently Do

All of these are things we can fully stand behind, because they are facts we record — not promises we make. Photograph every item and keep those photos as a permanent record. Write down each item's condition at every step — intake, cleaning, and return. Track where each item is and who handled it, with dates (the chain of custody). Store the owner's own receipts, purchase records, and any authenticity cards they already have. Clean, press, prepare, store, and ship items through trusted providers. Give the owner a complete record they can view anytime and take with them.

## 6. What We Say — and What We Never Say

| We can say | We never say |
| --- | --- |
| "Here is the documented history of your item while in our care." | "We have authenticated this item." |
| "These are the authenticity papers you provided." | "This item is genuine." |
| "You told us you paid this amount for it." | "This item is worth this amount." |
| "Here is the condition record at each step." | "Your collection is up this much this year." |

## 7. The Bottom Line

Saying no to these things costs LLV nothing it actually needs. The valuable thing — the trusted relationship and the complete, documented record of a client's belongings — is fully ours to keep and stand behind. Verifying that items are genuine and setting their value are services a larger partner can add later, because they have the experts and the legal protection to carry that risk. We keep the part that is valuable and safe, and we decline the part that is dangerous.
