Client Item Protection — Insurance & Liability Approach

## 1. The One Idea

Insuring a member's belongings and establishing what those belongings are worth are two separate things. LLV can fully protect client property without ever authenticating an item or assigning it a value. The protection comes from insurance on property in our care; the value always comes from the owner or a licensed third party — never from LLV. This keeps item protection squarely inside the Trust & Liability Guardrails.

## 2. The Vehicle — Bailee Insurance

"Bailee" coverage (also called warehouse legal liability or customers' goods coverage) is built precisely for a business that holds other people's property in its care, custody, and control. LLV's own policy covers loss of or damage to client items while they are in LLV custody and in transit, up to set limits. LLV is the insured; the member is protected through LLV's liability terms in the service agreement. This is the standard managed-logistics model, and it does not depend on LLV verifying authenticity or value.

## 3. Setting Value Without LLV Appraising

Coverage references a value, but LLV never sets it. The value is established one of these ways, each clearly labeled in the Vault: Owner-declared — the member states a value per item, recorded as client-provided. Client-supplied documentation — the member provides their own appraisal or purchase receipts, stored in the Vault (a third party's number, not LLV's). Scheduled on the member's own policy — for high-value pieces above a set threshold, the member schedules them on their own homeowner's/valuables coverage, with LLV's coverage sitting as secondary or capped.

## 4. What the Vault Contributes — Provability, Not Valuation

LLV's documentation does not say what an item is worth. What it does is make a claim provable: the intake and condition photos, the condition timeline, and the chain-of-custody record establish that the item existed, in what condition, and that it was in LLV's custody — exactly what an adjuster needs to settle a claim quickly. The documented Vault therefore makes the insurance work better and the loss rate lower, without LLV ever crossing into authentication or valuation.

## 5. Capping Exposure

Because LLV does not verify what it is holding, exposure is capped on three fronts: Per-item and per-client aggregate limits — working figures in the Assumptions Register are ~$5,000 per item and ~$50,000 per client; final limits set with the broker. A liability cap in the bailment/service agreement — tying LLV's liability to the documented value within those limits, so exposure is never open-ended. A high-value threshold — items above it are either scheduled on the member's own policy or excluded from LLV-borne coverage.

## 6. The Regulatory Line (Confirm Before Launch)

There is a real distinction to settle with a broker and counsel: (a) LLV bearing liability for items in its care, backed by LLV's bailee policy — clean, and the recommended structure. (b) LLV selling an insurance product to members — collecting premiums for coverage — which can require insurance-producer licensing. The planned "protection membership" should almost certainly be framed as an enhanced service / liability tier backed by LLV's coverage, not as LLV selling insurance. A commercial broker who writes bailee and cargo coverage can structure (a); counsel confirms the agreement language and the membership framing.

## 7. In-Transit Coverage

Carrier liability on standard shipments is minimal and does not protect high-value goods. Movement between corridor ends must be covered by transit/cargo bailee coverage as part of the same program, so the chain of protection is continuous from pickup to delivery.

## 8. What to Confirm With a Broker and Counsel

Bailee and general-liability program: per-occurrence and aggregate limits, per-item sub-limit, and the high-value threshold. Whether members must carry their own coverage for items above the threshold, and how LLV's coverage coordinates. In-transit/cargo coverage across the WI–AZ corridor and any provider hand-offs. Exclusions to expect and how they are disclosed to members. The bailment/service-agreement language: duty of care, liability cap, claims process and timeline, and the documented-value basis for settlement. The "protection membership" structure — liability tier vs. sold product — and any licensing implications.

## 9. Guardrail Alignment

Everything here holds to the Trust & Liability Guardrails: values are owner-declared or supplied by a licensed appraiser/insurer; LLV records them, clearly labeled, and never generates its own number; LLV authenticates nothing; and protection is delivered through insurance on property in custody, not through any claim about genuineness or worth.
